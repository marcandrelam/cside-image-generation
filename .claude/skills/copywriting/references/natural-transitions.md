# Natural Transitions Reference

Transition phrases that keep copy flowing without sounding robotic or AI-generated. Organized by function.

---

## The Problem with AI Transitions

AI models default to a small set of high-frequency connectors:
- "Furthermore," / "Moreover,"
- "In addition,"
- "It's worth noting that"
- "That being said,"
- "Notwithstanding,"
- "At its core,"
- "This, in turn,"

These patterns are immediately recognizable and break trust. Replace them with natural alternatives.

---

## Transitions by Function

### Adding information

**Avoid**: Furthermore, moreover, in addition, additionally, what's more

**Use instead**:
- "And —" (simple conjunction, often strongest)
- "Beyond that,"
- "On top of this,"
- "There's also the matter of"
- "Which brings up another point:"
- "[New idea]. [Supporting idea]." (no transition needed)
- "Not only that —" (use sparingly)

### Contrasting or qualifying

**Avoid**: However, that being said, notwithstanding, nevertheless, on the other hand

**Use instead**:
- "But —"
- "Except that,"
- "The catch:"
- "The problem:"
- "This doesn't mean"
- "Still,"
- "Then again,"
- "In practice, though,"
- "The reality is,"

### Cause and effect

**Avoid**: Therefore, thus, consequently, as a result, this, in turn

**Use instead**:
- "So —"
- "Which means"
- "That's why"
- "The result:"
- "This leads to"
- "And that's the problem."
- "Which is exactly the issue."

### Introducing examples

**Avoid**: For instance, for example (acceptable but overused), as an example

**Use instead**:
- "Take [X]."
- "Consider [scenario]."
- "Here's what that looks like:"
- "Picture this:"
- "Case in point:"
- "[Specific example without preamble]"

### Explaining or clarifying

**Avoid**: In other words, to put it differently, essentially, basically, at its core

**Use instead**:
- "Put simply:"
- "In plain terms:"
- "Translated: [plain version]"
- "[Restate with fewer words]"
- "Here's the short version:"

### Moving to solution or conclusion

**Avoid**: In conclusion, to summarize, in summary, to sum up, all things considered

**Use instead**:
- "The fix:"
- "Here's what changes that:"
- "The good news:"
- "That's where [product] comes in."
- "Which is exactly what [product] does."
- "[Jump straight into the solution without signposting]"

### Showing sequence or process

**Avoid**: Firstly, secondly, thirdly, subsequently, thereafter

**Use instead**:
- Numbered list (cleaner than numbered words)
- "Start with X. Then Y. Finally Z."
- "Step one is [X]. From there,"
- "Before you can [Y], you need [X]."

### Creating urgency or emphasis

**Avoid**: Crucially, importantly, it's important to note, it's worth mentioning

**Use instead**:
- "Here's the critical part:"
- "This is the one that matters:"
- "The part most teams miss:"
- "Pay attention to this:"
- [Just state the important thing — emphasis comes from placement, not signposting]

## Rhythm Techniques

### Vary sentence length deliberately

**Weak** (all same length):
```
Third-party scripts pose a significant security risk to e-commerce sites. Attackers
use compromised scripts to steal payment card data. The attack is often undetected
for months. Companies face significant fines and reputational damage.
```

**Strong** (varied rhythm):
```
Third-party scripts are your biggest unmonitored attack surface. Attackers compromise
a vendor's CDN — not your infrastructure — and inject card-skimming code silently.
You'd never know. Until your payment processor calls.
```

### The short sentence punch

Use a 3-5 word sentence after a longer one for impact:
- "Your quarterly audit caught nothing. It never does."
- "Detection takes 197 days on average. That's too long."
- "We built something different. Here's what that means."

### The colon setup

Use a colon to create anticipation:
- "Here's the thing about CSP: it only blocks what you tell it to block."
- "The PCI DSS 4.0.1 requirement is specific: you need real-time monitoring."
- "There's a better approach: behavioral baselining."

### The em dash alternative

Em dashes are the most overused AI punctuation mark. Replace them:

| Instead of | Use |
|---|---|
| Real-time monitoring — unlike batch scanning — catches changes immediately | Real-time monitoring, unlike batch scanning, catches changes immediately |
| This approach — which we developed over 3 years — handles edge cases | This approach (developed over three years) handles edge cases |
| Scripts — especially third-party ones — are your biggest risk | Scripts are your biggest risk, especially third-party ones |

### The rule of three

Groups of three land well. But avoid obvious triples:
- **Weak triple**: "fast, reliable, and secure" (cliché)
- **Strong triple**: "it's installed in 5 minutes, baselining in 48 hours, and alerting in real time within a week"

## Sentence Starters to Avoid

These are dead giveaways of AI-generated copy:

- "By [gerund]ing..." (By implementing..., By leveraging..., By ensuring...)
- "Whether you're a..." (Whether you're a developer, security team, or compliance officer...)
- "It's important to note that..."
- "Let's explore..."
- "In today's [anything] landscape..."
- "In the realm of..."
- "Gone are the days of..."

## Opening Techniques That Work

### Start with the reader's situation
"Your checkout page runs on 40+ third-party scripts."

### Start with a specific number or fact
"197 days. That's how long a script-based breach goes undetected on average."

### Start with a question the reader is already asking
"How do you audit scripts you didn't write?"

### Start with the consequence
"A compromised analytics tag quietly streamed card data for four months before anyone noticed."

### Start with a contrarian claim
"Your CSP isn't enough."

### Start mid-story
"A team of two caught a Magecart attack in 47 seconds. Here's how."
