# Plain English Alternatives

Replace complex, formal, or AI-generated vocabulary with plain English equivalents. Organized by category.

---

## The Core Rule

If there is a simpler word that means the same thing, use it. Plain language is not dumbing down — it is respecting the reader's time.

## Words That Add No Meaning (Cut Them)

Remove these without replacing — they add length without adding meaning:

**Intensifiers to cut**: absolutely, basically, certainly, clearly, definitely, essentially, extremely, fundamentally, incredibly, naturally, obviously, quite, really, significantly, simply, surely, truly, ultimately, undoubtedly, very

**Empty qualifiers to cut**: somewhat, rather, fairly, pretty, kind of, sort of, a bit

**Throat-clearing phrases to cut**:
- "It is worth noting that..."
- "It should be mentioned that..."
- "Needless to say..."
- "As a matter of fact..."
- "For all intents and purposes..."
- "In terms of..."
- "The fact that..."

---

## How to Use This Reference

1. **When editing**: Flag complex words first, then look up alternatives here
2. **When writing**: Keep this open in a second tab — catch complex words before they go in
3. **For AI-generated text**: Run a quick scan for words in these tables — AI models have a strong preference for the complex column
4. **For technical audiences**: Some jargon is appropriate — only substitute when the audience may not know the term

The goal is not to eliminate all technical language. It is to use the simplest word that accurately conveys the meaning to the target audience.
